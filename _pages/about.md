#### **Who I am & What I do**

I am a palaeoclimatologist focused on developing and applying statistical techniques based on the estimation and propagation of proxy natural uncertainties to reconstruct climate from extensive networks of fossil pollen data. In particular, I developed a reconstruction technique called CREST, whose modelling assumptions are specifically tailored to the constraints associated with the rich and diverse vegetation of the tropics. My research also focuses on the data-model interface and aims to capitalise on the relative strengths of each asset to better understand past climate dynamics. With these tools, I study the spatial imprint of climate change in the tropics on the centennial to glacial-interglacial time scales.


#### **Why I do it**

Quantitative estimates of past climate are essential to characterise past climate change and evaluate Earth System Model (ESM) simulations. However, recent data compilation studies showed that the quality and quantity of quantified climate reconstructions available from the tropical and southern temperate regions are dramatically limited compared to what is available from the Northern Hemisphere extratropics and, to some extent, Antarctica. Our understanding of past climate variability in these regions is thus extremely limited, and past and future climate simulations are very uncertain. While fundamentally problematic for our knowledge of climate variability in these regions, these “quantified data gaps” also significantly impact and bias our understanding of the global climate system dynamics as a whole. In the context of recent and future climate change, there is an urgent need to obtain more and better climate reconstructions from understudied regions. Better constraining past climate change in such regions is thus critical to provide essential pieces of the puzzle and help decipher how and when the different elements of the global climate system are connected in space and time.
